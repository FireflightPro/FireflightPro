Finding Your Words

A facilitated session for aligning on the language of Earthsight AI Consortium — before design, before social media, before anything else goes public.

Use Before Visual Branding Retreat (Apr 21)
Format Group — 4 Consortium Members
Total Time ~90 Minutes
Facilitator Ariel Strong

Why verbal clarity has to come before visual identity

Logos, colors, and layouts are all expressions of a message. Without a clear, shared message underneath, visual design becomes decoration — it looks good but doesn't say anything specific. When four people with four specialties are trying to present as one organization, the words have to come first.

Your group's specific challenge: you're all technically fluent, which means your natural language skews toward what you do (services, tools, methods) rather than what changes for the people you help. This session is designed to close that gap — to get you to plain, specific, human language that a Flagstaff restaurant owner or school principal would immediately understand and respond to.

Everything produced here feeds directly into the April 21 visual session. Walk in with your words locked, and the branding work becomes dramatically faster.

✦
Pre-Work — Before the Meeting
Individual — 5 minutes per person

Each member writes this down — by hand or in a note — before arriving. Do not talk to each other about it first. Uncoordinated first drafts are more honest and more revealing than polished group answers.

Write Your Answers To:
  • 1
    In one sentence: What does Earthsight AI Consortium do?
  • 2
    In one sentence: Who is it for?
  • 3
    In one sentence: What's different about your life or work after Earthsight helps you?
Facilitator (Ariel): Collect these before the session starts. Read all four sets. Underline every word that appears in more than one person's answer — those are your through-lines. Circle every piece of jargon or technical language. Bring both lists to Activity 1. Do not share the answers with the group yet — wait until everyone is together.
1
The First Draft — Hear Yourselves
Group — 15 minutes

Before you can fix your language, you need to hear what you're actually saying. This activity gets the unfiltered first drafts into the room so the group can see where they agree, where they diverge, and where the jargon hides.

How to Run It
  • 1
    Read each person's three answers aloud, one at a time — no commentary yet. Just listen.
  • 2
    On the whiteboard, write two columns: SHARED LANGUAGE (words or phrases that appeared in multiple answers) and JARGON WATCH (technical terms, acronyms, or insider language a non-technical person might not understand).
  • 3
    Give the group 2 minutes to call out additions to both columns. No debate — just capture everything.
  • 4
    Ask: if a neighbor read these answers, what questions would they still have? Write those questions down — they reveal your messaging gaps.

⚠ Common Jargon to Watch For — Replace Before You Go Public

LLM→AI assistant
AI implementation→putting AI to work in your business
prompt engineering→learning to communicate with AI
AI-forward→ready for what's coming
generative AI→AI that creates — text, images, ideas
AI ecosystem→the range of AI tools available
sustainable AI practices→using AI responsibly and safely
consortium→a team of local AI experts (keep "consortium" for formal use only)
What you're listening for: The moment someone says something that makes the rest of the group say "yes — that's it." That phrasing is your gold. Write it down exactly as spoken. Natural speech is almost always more accessible than written language.
2
The "So What?" Ladder
Group — 20 minutes

Technical communicators describe what they do. Effective communicators describe what changes for the people they help. The "So What?" ladder forces you down from service description to human outcome — the level where your message actually lands.

How to Run It
  • 1
    Choose the most jargon-heavy or technical sentence from Activity 1. Write it on the board — this is Rung 1.
  • 2
    Ask the group: "So what does that mean for the person you're helping?" Write the answer on Rung 2.
  • 3
    Ask again: "And so what does that mean for them?" Rung 3.
  • 4
    Repeat until the answer is something a non-technical person would say in real life. Usually 3–4 rungs. The bottom rung is almost always the truest version of your message.
  • 5
    Run the ladder a second time with a different starting sentence — one from a different member's specialty. Compare the bottom rungs. Are you arriving at similar places?

Example to demonstrate the technique if the group gets stuck: Start with "We provide AI implementation services for SMEs in the wildland-urban interface." So what? → "We help small businesses use AI tools in their work." So what? → "They save time and stop doing the same tasks manually every week." So what? → "They can focus on what actually matters in their business instead of drowning in busy work." That last sentence is the message. Notice it has no jargon and a non-technical person would nod immediately.
3
The Neighbor Test — 60 Seconds, No Notes
Individual + Group — 20 minutes

Natural speech is usually more accessible than written language. This test captures how each of you actually talks about Earthsight when you're not in "professional mode" — and that spoken version is often closer to your real message than anything written.

How to Run It
  • 1
    Each person takes 60 seconds to explain Earthsight to an imaginary non-technical neighbor at a backyard barbecue. No notes. Record each one on a phone or laptop. (Do it one at a time so others can listen.)
  • 2
    Play each recording back. As a group, listen for: words that keep appearing across all four recordings, moments where the explanation suddenly clicked into plain language, and anything that made someone in the room nod or lean in.
  • 3
    Write the standout phrases on the whiteboard. These are your raw material.
  • 4
    Ask: which of these recordings would make a Flagstaff restaurant owner pick up the phone? That recording wins. Why? What did it do differently?
Capture Here — Phrases Worth Keeping
Member 1 — standout phrase: _______________
Member 2 — standout phrase: _______________
Member 3 — standout phrase: _______________
Member 4 — standout phrase: _______________
Words that appeared in 3+ recordings: _______________
Why this works: The 60-second constraint prevents over-engineering. The barbecue framing lowers the emotional stakes. People almost always find a phrase in their spoken version that they'd never write — and that phrase is usually the most authentic one.
4
The Live Claude Synthesis — Use Your Own Tools
Group — 15 minutes

This is the meta-moment: use AI to synthesize your own messaging, in real time, on a shared screen. It's fast, removes ego from the process (no one is attached to Claude's phrasing), and — not incidentally — is a live demonstration of exactly what you teach others to do.

How to Run It
  • 1
    Each person writes 3–4 sentences describing: (a) what they personally bring to Earthsight, and (b) who they help and how. This takes 3 minutes.
  • 2
    Paste all four descriptions into Claude using the prompt below. Project the screen so everyone sees the output simultaneously.
  • 3
    Read the output aloud. Mark anything that feels right with a ✓, anything that feels off with an ✗. Ask: what did Claude get right that we haven't said to each other yet?
  • 4
    Iterate once — give Claude the marked output and ask it to adjust based on your reactions.

5
We Help X Do Y So That Z — Lock the Framework
Group — 20 minutes

This is your core positioning sentence — the sentence that, once locked, governs everything from your social media bio to your grant applications to how each member introduces themselves at events. The "so that Z" clause is the hardest part, and it's also the most important: it forces you off service description and onto human outcome.

How to Run It
  • 1
    Start with the three provocations below. React to them together — mark what's right, what's wrong, what's missing. Do not try to polish one of these. Use them as targets to push against.
  • 2
    Write five of your own versions of the framework. Quantity over quality at first — go fast. No one edits mid-flight.
  • 3
    Read each version aloud. The group votes: closer or further? Iterate toward the version that makes everyone feel seen and accurately described.
  • 4
    Test the winner: would a Flagstaff restaurant owner understand it? Would it work on a grant application? Would your mom understand what you do from this one sentence? It needs to pass all three.

The Framework

We help who you serve do what they can do with you so that what changes in their life or work .

Tip: "so that" should describe a change the person cares about — not a service you deliver. If "so that" sounds like something you do, push one level further.

Three Starter Provocations — React to These, Don't Refine Them
Version A — Community Focused
"We help Northern Arizona businesses, schools, and organizations understand and use AI confidently, so our community thrives as the world changes around it."
Strong on geography and community outcome. May be too broad — "businesses, schools, and organizations" is almost everyone.
Version B — Outcome Focused
"We help Flagstaff's people and organizations get ahead of the AI curve — practically, safely, and on their own terms."
Punchy and human. "On their own terms" does a lot of work. May need more specificity for grant audiences.
Version C — Full Spectrum
"We are Flagstaff's end-to-end AI partner — educating students, training workers, building tools, and connecting our community to an AI-powered future that works for everyone."
Covers all four specialties explicitly. Longer, but clear. Good for grant applications and formal introductions. Too long for social bios.
